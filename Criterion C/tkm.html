Competeing

Not Cooperative, Very Assertive

In this method an individual in a group, pursues their own interest rather than keeping group interests in mind. This is done at the expense of a good outcome or of another group member. It can be a rather selfish approach, but if the person 'competing' isn't being heard, then its a good way to get their point across


Collaborating

Very Cooperative, Very Assertive

Collaborating is to work together with group memebers and come up with a solution jointly, to produce an excellent outcome and learn from each other. Out of all the methods, this one encorages fruitful communication the most

Compromising

A Perfect balance of Cooperativeness and Assertiveness

Compromising is when conflciting parties each make their own compromises and come to a joint solution while prioritizing the task they have been assigned over the argument they've been having. Its finding a middle-ground in the situation and working from there. Fairly similar to collaborating.

Avoiding

Not Cooperative, Not Assertive

As the name would suggest, avoiding is simply ignoring the conflict in the hopes that it will resolve itself. This approach does work to some extent, but only in situations where members can't deal with the conflict, mostly due to time constraints. But sometimes conflicts are like snowballs rolling down a hill. They'll just get bigger over time, so its best to avoid, avoiding it when you can.

Accommodating

Very Cooperative, Not Assertive

Resolving conflicts by putting ones needs above your own. This is a rather quick method to resolving a conflict, but it can be unsatisfying simultaneously. While resolving conflicts if only one person is happy in the outcome, then productivity and motivation can be lowered. But it's best to keep in mind that when there are time constraints, it's better to be the bigger person and carry on.

The Thomas Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument

Home > Introduction to Conflict Resolution > The Thomas-Kilmann Model


The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument, developed by Kenneth W. Thomas and Ralph Kilmann, is a model that lists 5 strategies to deal with conflict in a situation and it rates them on a scale of assertiveness and cooperativeness.

The 5 measures are Avoiding, Accommodating, Competeing, Collaborating and Compromising.

Its more commonly used in corporate settings and to determine a persons style of conflict resolution, but it can be applied in multiple situations.
